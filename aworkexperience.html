Cisco

Interning as Cisco was a life-changing experience.

I grew so much interning at Cisco. My interpersonal, soft, technical, problem solving, and time management skills all saw a considerable growth spurt. I was surrounded by so many talented, passionate, and supportive people. Although I learned many lessons, the number one thing that I learned is to create a voice for myself and to ask for what I want.

Bringing People Together

Before I get to what I was working on, I really want to highlight the great times I had with the intern cohort. Work is work obviously, but I think it's equally important to have fun and connect with others at work. It's safe to say my summer at Cisco was a summer I will never forget. I went sky-diving, became an Intern Ambassador, and most importantly, I woke up everyday looking forward to my day.

Projects

I worked on several projects when I was at Cisco. The way the intern program is structured is that interns have a main project with the option of taking on several stretch assignments. This was no different for me.

Although my title may say otherwise, the projects I took on required the skills of a software engineer, data scientist, and analyst. My main project was based on a case study. At a base level, Cisco's business is selling enterprise level networking hardware and software. Since Cisco outsources all of their manufacturing to third parties, they have a lot of people in Supply Chain to make sure all the processes run smoothly.

Project 1: Internal Dashboard Tool for Excess and Obsolete

To put it simply, a lot of processes for mitigating excess and obsolete parts were extremely manual. The crux of the issue was that major stakeholders would often miss out on major opportunities to mitigate parts because they would take so much time gathering and analyzing information. Since my job title is "Project Manager" and my career goals are to move into product, I wanted to structure this project to how a product team would ship a product in the real world. So this means having excellent stakeholder management, adhering to the SDLC, conducting market research, and most importantly, creating something of value for Cisco. Now I may not be able to describe too much in detail, my tool was built in a two step process of automating the gathering of data and then automating the analysis portion of it. I directly wanted to tackle the crux of the issue and highlighted that when I presented the product with my team on Cisco Live TV.

Project 2: Increasing Accurcy of Demand Forecasting AI

Now there have already been talks about how large companies are starting to implement AI into their work structure, and Cisco is no different. This project was a stretch assignment of mine that I wanted to take on to learn more about AI and the underlying processes behind it. Other interns had stretch assignments of building AI chatbots for the Cisco website, but I really wanted to understand how AI/ML worked. I don't traditionally come from a data science or statistics background, but I was really interested and so this project was where I really took on the attitude of asking for what I want.

Project 3: Documenting Process Flow for Cisco Meraki

This project was near the end of my internship, but also involved major stakeholders. Essentially, this project consisted of conducting internal research on Cisco Meraki's supply chain, mapping out the process flows, and making comparisons against Cisco's own flows. I pointed out where the differences lied and how either company could improve processes within their respective supply chains.

Key Takeaways

  • Don't be afraid to ask for help
  • Always keep others in mind
  • Giving updates and showing empathy matters
  • Market yourself and connect with others

Jabil

Working at Jabil really opened my eyes to the field of software development and how to navigate a large company structure. I was brought on board a bit later than the rest of the intern cohort, but here is where I learned to be a self-starter and to do things on my own. This is the attitude I carried with my to Cisco and why I took on the stretch assignments that I did.

No one at Jabil was expecting me to help out with this project. The migration to the new ERP was my only responsiblity, but during my breaks I would wander around the building. Since I started a bit later, I wasn't as close with the initial intern cohort, but I still made small talk from time to time. Anyway, this one day as I was wandering, I noticed a couple of mechanical keyboards poking out of the door. It was a hobby I had recently picked up and from the glance I had, they looked like really nice keyboards. From there, I made friends with the software development manager and asked what project he was working on. We hit if off immediately and not long after, I found myself immersed with the experience I had been seeking.

Project 1: Migration to new MES

This was my main project, which was ensuring quality data migration of the transition of the new MES (Manufacturing Execution System) which was tied to their existing ERP. I was brought on board for 6 weeks, in which my manager didn't expect to finish as he had scheduled this part of the project to be 9 weeks. I had created data maps for the facility assets and how that would be translated into the new MES and cleaned the data of redudancies.

Project 2: Feature Launch of Internal Software

Since the product being built was an internal software and the development team was small, it was a lot easier to brainstorm ideas and get the ball rolling. It was really cool to get the chance to work on something that would be used by the individuals I would be interacting with. I managed the development and launch of three features for their internal project management software, implementing employee verification with Okta Sessions API, creating a dyanmic timeline where you can block tasks, and a ticket issuing system akin to Jira.

Key Takeaways

  • Don't be afraid to start conversations with people outside of your business function
  • Make yourself stand out by going the extra mile!
